Termite Warning Signs in Wyoming, MI

Home / Blog / Termite Warning Signs in Wyoming, MI

Termites are often called silent destroyers, and for good reason. These insects steadily consume the wood inside your walls, floors, and structural framing long before any visible sign appears. By the time most homeowners in Wyoming, MI notice a problem, the colony may have been active for months or even years. Early detection is the single most important factor in limiting termite damage, and knowing what to look for can save you thousands of dollars in repairs. This guide from Wyoming Pest Control walks you through the warning signs every homeowner should recognize.

Mud Tubes Along Your Foundation

Subterranean termites — the most common type in Wyoming — build pencil-width mud tubes to travel between their underground nest and your home's wood. These tubes protect the termites from open air, which dries them out, and from predators. You will most often find mud tubes running up foundation walls, along crawl space piers, or near the base of exterior siding. If you discover a mud tube, break a small section open. If it is rebuilt within a few days, the colony is active and feeding.

Drywood termites, by contrast, live entirely inside the wood they consume and do not build mud tubes. This is one reason a professional inspection matters: identifying the termite type determines the correct treatment approach.

Discarded Wings and Swarmers

In spring and early summer, mature termite colonies release winged reproductive termites called swarmers. These swarmers leave the nest to start new colonies, and they are frequently the first visible evidence of an infestation. Swarmers are drawn to light, so you may notice them around windows and doors. Shortly after swarming, they shed their wings, leaving small piles of identical, translucent wings on windowsills, in spider webs, or near light fixtures.

Homeowners often mistake termite swarmers for flying ants. The difference matters: termite swarmers have straight antennae, a uniform waist, and two pairs of equal-length wings, while ants have bent antennae, a pinched waist, and unequal wing lengths. If you are unsure, save a few specimens and have an experienced technician identify them.

Hollow-Sounding or Blistering Wood

Termites consume wood from the inside out, leaving a thin layer of surface material intact. As a result, infested wood may look normal but sound hollow when tapped. Trim, baseboards, door frames, and structural beams that produce a papery or empty sound are worth investigating. You may also notice that wood surfaces appear blistered or darkened, which can indicate galleries just beneath the surface or moisture from termite activity.

Floors and ceilings affected by termites can sag or feel spongy underfoot. While these symptoms can also point to water damage, both problems warrant immediate professional attention.

Frass: Termite Droppings

Drywood termites push their droppings, called frass, out of small kick-out holes in the wood they infest. Frass looks like tiny, ridged pellets and often resembles coarse sawdust or coffee grounds. Finding small mounds of frass beneath wooden furniture, window frames, or structural members is a strong indicator of an active drywood colony nearby.

Why Early Detection Matters in Wyoming

Termites cause billions of dollars in property damage across the country every year, and most of that damage is not covered by standard homeowner's insurance. The longer a colony feeds undetected, the more extensive and expensive the structural repairs become. In Wyoming, MI, warm conditions and moisture-rich soil create ideal habitat for subterranean colonies, which means regular inspections are a smart investment for any property owner.

A professional termite inspection examines the areas most homeowners cannot easily access, including crawl spaces, attics, and the foundation perimeter.
